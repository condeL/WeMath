1.0 Surds

1.1 Addition, subtraction and multiplication of surds 

1.1.1 Simplifying surds 

Consider the square root of the number 6. We know that 2 × 3 is 6 and so √6 can be written as √(2×3). Therefore, the square root of 6 can be expressed as the product of the square roots of 2 and 3. That is √(6 ) = √2 × √3. Similarly, √12  = √(4 ×3) = √4 × √3 = 2√3. In general, If k = mn, then √k √mn = √m √n.

If √n is such that n can be expressed as the product of a perfect square and another integer, then √n is said to be a reducible surd. For example, since 12 can be expressed as a product of the perfect square 4 and the number 3, √12 is a reducible surd.

Example 2.1 Simplify the following 

(a) √20, 

(b) √45, 

(c) √18, 

(d) √32, 

(e) √63.

Solution 

(a) √20 =  √(4 ×5) = √4 × √5= 2√5, 

(b) √45  = √9*5  = √(9 ) *  √5 = 3 √5, 

(c) √18 = √(9 ×2) = √9 * √2 = 3 √2, 

(d) √32 = √(16 ×2) = √16 * √2 = 4 √2, 

(e) √63 = √(9 ×7) = √9 * √7 = 3 √7.
